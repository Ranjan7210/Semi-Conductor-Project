The Hall Effect

The Hall Effect was discovered by Edwin Hall in 1879. It occurs when a magnetic field is applied perpendicular to the flow of electric current in a conductor or semiconductor. This creates a measurable transverse voltage that allows us to determine the density and polarity of charge carriers.

Physics & Derivation

When a magnetic field (B) is applied perpendicularly to a current (I), the charge carriers with drift velocity (vd) experience a Lorentz force:

FB = q(vd × B)

This sideways force displaces the charge carriers, causing them to accumulate on one transverse edge of the material. This accumulation generates an opposing Hall Electric Field (EH).

At steady-state equilibrium, the electric force perfectly balances the magnetic force:

FE = FB  →  qEH = q vd B  →  EH = vd B

Since the electric field relates to the potential difference (the Hall Voltage VH) across the width (w), we know EH = VH / w. Substituting the drift velocity vd = I / (n q A) = I / (n q w d) yields the core equation:

VH = (I × B) / (n × q × d)
  • B = Magnetic field (Tesla)
  • I = Current (Ampere)
  • n = Charge carrier density (m⁻³)
  • q = Elementary charge (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
  • d = Thickness of the material (m)

Real-world Applications

  • Material Characterization: Measuring the Hall Coefficient (RH = 1/nq) verifies whether a semiconductor is n-type (electrons) or p-type (holes).
  • Hall Effect Sensors: Used extensively in smartphones to detect magnetic fields (compasses).
  • Position Encoders & Switches: Contactless rotational speed measurement (e.g., in anti-lock braking systems).
  • Current Measurement: Used safely measure large currents in circuits without cutting the wires.
